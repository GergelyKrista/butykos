1. Executive Summary

Drinkustry is in good shape. Phase 7B is complete in Godot 4.2 with a working tycoon loop, a dual-layer map, save/load, and — in the latest build — a first pass at a tech tree (currently profit gates new buildings). That tech tree is a perfect substrate to grow into the design described below.

We are converging on a clear directional shift:

  • Theme: cyberpunk reskin (no code rewrite — the systems are theme-agnostic).
  • Differentiator: mega-corp factionalism, with each corp playable as a distinct vertical slice of the game.
  • Mode: four-corp asymmetric co-op is the design we're committing to and prototyping. Solo playability is deliberately left open — likely co-op-only, but to be revisited as the design matures. Competitive mode remains a possible v2 bonus.
  • Tension: two coupled mechanics — short-term interference (pollution, waste, water scarcity) and long-term shared tech tree + shared credits — force genuine cooperation rather than parallel solitaire.

Crucially, this is additive to existing systems, not a rewrite. With the four-corp direction now agreed, the next meeting should focus on the smallest first slice we can prototype and on which design questions can safely wait.

3. Strategic Direction

Theme: cyberpunk pivot

The original brewery/distillery framing stays mechanically intact, but the visual language and naming move toward near-future / cyberpunk megacorp. Mindustry is the closest tonal reference we already have. The code does not care about theme; this is a pure art and naming pass.

Differentiator: corporate factionalism

Rather than "the player runs a generic tycoon empire," each player becomes a corporation with a tightly scoped role. This frames every other decision in the project (UI, tech tree, art language, MP architecture).

Mode: four-corp co-op as the design centre

Co-op is the v1 target. Competitive mode is acknowledged as desirable but explicitly deferred — it can ship later as a reskin of the same systems with conflicting goals. Designing for co-op first dodges the hardest balance problems (PvP demands symmetric tuning; co-op tolerates asymmetric tuning), and matches what the four-corp design naturally wants to be.

The SP-vs-MP discussion — and where we landed

We circled this for a while. The core tension we identified:

  • "If the game is playable solo, why would players play it together?" — In Satisfactory, Mindustry, Factorio, and Age of Empires, multiplayer doesn't change the mechanics. Those games are solo games with multiplayer netcode bolted on. They're not co-op games in essence.
  • Deep Rock Galactic sits in a middle position: difficulty scales with players, the experience changes with company, but the missions are still solo-doable. It's co-op-leaning, not co-op-essential.
  • The four-corp design is structurally co-op-essential. The asymmetric roles, the negotiation around the shared tech tree, the "guys, can we deliver this order?" moments — these mechanics require multiple agents. Trying to retrofit a real solo mode means either (a) one player runs all four corps with role-switching (which collapses the differentiation back into a generic tycoon) or (b) AI plays the other three corps (which is a massive engineering effort, and AI corps can't replicate the negotiation that is the game).

Gergely's marketing concern, and the counter

Gergely's main objection: a co-op-only game is harder to market than one that's also playable solo. That is a real concern — it does close off the "buy on impulse, play alone" segment of the market, which is the largest segment for tycoon and factory games.

The counter, which we're leaning into: a four-corp asymmetric co-op tycoon is something we haven't really seen on the market. The tycoon-solo space is brutally saturated (Factorio, Satisfactory, Mindustry, Anno, Captain of Industry, dozens more). The true asymmetric co-op tycoon space is essentially empty. Releasing a co-op-only design isn't a constraint — it's a market position. Indie successes like It Takes Two, Overcooked, Lovers in a Dangerous Spacetime, and We Were Here show the pattern: when the design genuinely requires co-op, "you must play with friends" becomes the marketing hook, not the obstacle. Group purchases, streaming visibility, and word-of-mouth from gaming groups become the unit economics.

Where we landed (provisional)

We're committing to the four-corp co-op design as the prototype direction. Whether and how to add solo playability is a question we're explicitly deferring — to be revisited once the design has been validated in playtest. The lean is toward co-op-only as a deliberate market position, but we're not closing the door on solo-friendly options if the design naturally allows them.

A possible middle ground worth keeping in mind
Even if the shipping mode is co-op-only, a solo training / tutorial mode — where you play one corp solo against scripted scenarios with the other three as scripted partners — could serve four purposes without compromising the co-op design: (1) onboarding new players before they join a real session, (2) offering something to single-player buyers, (3) showcasing the game on streams or YouTube without needing a four-streamer collab, (4) practising your role before a friend's session. This is not a real SP game; it's a solo training mode. Different design intent, much smaller scope. Worth parking for now, returning to once we know what each corp's solo-playable kernel looks like.

4. The Four Corps — Player Roles

Each corp owns a vertical slice of the game and depends on the others. No corp can win alone; every corp has agency, identity, and meaningful decisions.

Corp 01 · Agricultural

The grower. Inputs to the chain.

Builds and operates everything that turns land into raw biological output. Farms, orchards, silos, irrigation systems. The agri player optimises yield against a multi-variable land-management puzzle.

What the player builds

  • Crop fields, orchards, livestock pastures (TBD scope)
  • Silos and on-site storage
  • Irrigation systems (depend on logistics-supplied water mains)

What the player manages

  • Soil chemistry: pH, nitrogen levels per tile
  • Water: field-level water levels; water is a globally scarce resource the whole table competes for
  • Suitable land: different crops and orchards demand different soil types — siting decisions matter
  • Yield maximisation across all of the above

Key dependencies (this is where co-op pressure lives)

  • Receives water mains from the logistics corp
  • Suffers from pollution caused by industrial / logistics / business activity
  • Sends raw produce out via the logistics corp to industrial processing
Corp 02 · Industrial

The processor. Recipes and machinery.

Builds the production buildings on the strategic map and steps inside them to design the machinery chain — Mindustry / Modulus Factory Automation style. The industrial player owns recipes, throughput, and byproducts.

What the player builds

  • Production buildings on the strategic map (e.g., breweries, distilleries, refineries)
  • Inside each building: the actual production line — machines, conveyors, intermediate steps
  • Example: a "Beer Brewery" main building exists, but which beer it produces and which machine chain makes it is up to the industrial player

What the player manages

  • Recipes and product variants
  • Input resource pacing (matched to what logistics can deliver)
  • Output product pacing (matched to what business can sell)
  • Byproducts — sewage, slag, emissions — that have to go somewhere

Key dependencies

  • Inputs arrive via logistics; if logistics can't keep up, factories starve
  • Outputs leave via logistics to selling points run by business
  • Byproducts cause pollution / waste interference on agri tiles unless properly handled
  • Power and water consumption depend on logistics-built grids
Corp 03 · Logistics

The connector. Everything between A and B.

Builds and operates the infrastructure that ties the world together. Without logistics, the other corps cannot interact — which is exactly why this player has so much leverage.

What the player builds

  • Roads, rails, vehicles, vehicle depots
  • Water mains and sewage pipes
  • Electricity lines and substations

What the player manages

  • Throughput across the network — bottlenecks become other corps' problems instantly
  • Vehicle fleets, routing, depot capacity
  • Maintenance / breakdowns (TBD scope)
  • Network expansion priorities under shared-tech budget pressure

Key dependencies

  • All other corps need logistics to do anything; this is the natural negotiation hub of the table
  • Funded — like everyone — through the shared credit pool managed (operationally) by business
Corp 04 · Business & Marketing

The dealmaker. Money, market, and reputation.

Handles the abstract layer — capital, contracts, sales, reputation. Less hands-on building, more strategy and external-facing decisions. Reference points: RollerCoaster Tycoon ad campaigns and Transport Tycoon contract logic.

What the player builds

  • Selling points / trading posts (where end products actually leave the system for money)
  • Branded outlets — set up product mix per outlet

What the player manages

  • Money & loans: can take loans, can extend internal loans to other corps when needed (creates a real interpersonal mechanic)
  • NPC trading: third-party buyers and sellers, market dynamics, prices that drift over time
  • Sales pricing: per-product, per-outlet
  • Orders / contracts: NPC orders for X units of product Y by date Z — this is the canonical "guys, can we do this?" moment that drives table conversation
  • Marketing: ad campaigns, government bribes, sales / discount events — i.e. the RCT/TT toolkit

Key dependencies

  • Cannot sell without industrial producing and logistics delivering
  • Owns the revenue side of the shared credit pool — the rest of the table feels their decisions immediately
Player count target
The 4-corp design implies a 4-player co-op target. Worth deciding explicitly: do we ship 4-player or do we collapse two corps (e.g., logistics + business as one role) for a 3-player ship and split later? See decision D-04.

5. Cross-Corp Tension Mechanics

Without explicit tension mechanics, asymmetric co-op tends to drift into parallel solitaire — three or four players doing their own thing and waving at each other across the map. We design two coupled mechanics on different timescales to prevent this.

Short-term: interference
Each corp's actions create externalities for the others — sometimes negative — that have to be managed in real time.
  • Industrial waste / emissions reduce agri yield on nearby tiles
  • Agri runoff and land use constrain where industrial can build cleanly
  • Logistics bottlenecks throttle everyone's throughput
  • Business pricing or marketing decisions can swing demand mid-run, leaving the others scrambling
  • Water is the cleanest shared-scarcity lever — agri irrigation and industrial process water both pull from the same pool, mediated by logistics-built mains
Long-term: shared tech tree + shared credits
Building directly on Gergely's existing profit-gated tech tree, all players research from one tree using one shared budget.
  • Every research decision becomes a group negotiation
  • "Do we unlock industrial tier 2 or agri's drought-resistant crops first?"
  • Forces strategic conversation about pacing, not just spatial coordination
  • Naturally throttles the runaway-leader problem
  • Credits also fund infrastructure spend, so the same pool services research and capital expansion — opportunity cost everywhere

The two mechanics are deliberately on different timescales. Interference is the moment-to-moment "your factory is poisoning my farm right now" pressure; shared tech is the strategic "we agreed last round you'd get the next unlock, but the bottleneck has shifted" pressure. Together they create a multi-timescale dialogue that gives co-op real texture.

Optional refinement: per-corp private R&D

To preserve corp identity and avoid the "shared pool eats everything" failure (see section 6), each corp could also have a small private R&D budget — generated by their own internal performance — usable only on that corp's intra-corp tech. The shared pool is for cross-corp infrastructure and major unlocks; the private budget is for your own corp's internal flavor. To discuss.

6. Co-op Failure Modes & Mitigations

Co-op design has a small, well-known set of failure patterns. Naming them up front so we can design against them — and watch for them in playtest.

Failure mode What it looks like Mitigation
Alpha player problem One experienced player ends up directing everyone else's research and routing. Other players become spectators. Per-corp private R&D budgets; veto power on research that primarily benefits another corp; mechanics that genuinely require local knowledge each player has but others don't.
Decision deadlock Three or four players argue forever about which tech to research; the game stalls. Proposal + countdown + auto-pass-if-no-objection rhythm; explicit vote only when someone objects.
Left-behind corp One corp's tech branch never gets credits because it isn't the current bottleneck. That player watches their tree starve. Per-session credit-allocation tracking visible in UI; soft floor on per-corp progression; private R&D safety net.
Parallel solitaire Players each tend their own corner; co-op feels like four single-player games sharing a screen. The interference + shared-tech combo is the structural answer; orders / contracts from business are the social trigger that pulls everyone into one conversation.

7. Additional Design Branches Discussed

Three further proposals came up in conversation. Each represents a design choice that genuinely affects the shape of v1, and each is worth recording explicitly so the next meeting can either ratify or push back.

7.1 More corps to choose from (Gergely's idea)

Gergely raised whether to expand beyond four corps for replayability. The instinct: more options = more variety per session.

The objection (Ambrus): the four-corp design is built around a complete partition of the game into essential roles. Adding more corps as new roles either sub-divides existing ones into anemic pieces, or invents overlap that creates ownership ambiguity. Both weaken the core design.

Where we landed — sub-types emerge from play, not picked at session start

Rather than picking a faction at the start (Civilization-style), each corp starts from the same baseline and develops into a sub-type through play. The two drivers:

  • Corp-exclusive tech tree / upgrade branches — the choices a player makes inside their own tech tree shape their corp's identity over the run. An Agri player who keeps investing in greenhouse and biotech research becomes a "BioCorp" run; one who invests in heritage seed lines and slow-cycle resilience becomes a "Heritage" run. Same starting position, different stories.
  • Narrative event decisions (see 7.3) — corp-exclusive event choices nudge the corp's identity further, sometimes opening or locking branches that wouldn't otherwise be available.

Sketch identities the system should be capable of producing (not as picked options, but as emergent run flavors):

  • Agri: BioCorp · Heritage Co-op · Vertical Towers
  • Industrial: Refinery Specialist · Modular Manufacturing · Heavy Machinery
  • Logistics: Ground Network · Rail Specialist · Pipeline & Utility
  • Business: Mass Market · Luxury Brand · Black Market / Gray Economy

Why emergent > picked for this design:

  • No faction-select screen friction or analysis paralysis at session start
  • Same player picking the same role twice still gets meaningfully different runs
  • Every choice in the corp-exclusive tech tree becomes identity-defining, not just optimal-or-not
  • Events can alter a corp's trajectory mid-run — your story can pivot
  • Smaller art surface area: no need for 12 distinct visual identities — four corp baselines with modular modifier accents (logos, palettes, building skins) earned through play

Recommendation: yes to more variety, no to more roles. Replayability lives in the shared tech tree + corp-exclusive tech tree + narrative events trio — each session writes its own corp identity through play, rather than picking one from a menu.

Caveat to design carefully: the corp-exclusive tech tree now has to be deep and branching enough to deliver on this. If the corp tree only has a handful of nodes, every Agri run looks the same. Worth scoping the tree size with sub-type emergence as the explicit design target — probably 3–4 meaningful branching points per corp at v1.

7.2 Combat mechanic (Gergely's idea, with pushback)

Gergely floated adding combat. Ambrus pushed back: this would change the core genre into tower defense or RTS like AoE2. That instinct is correct.

Adding combat to a four-corp co-op tycoon either:

  • Overloads one corp — industrial would become "factory + military," diluting both
  • Requires a fifth role — breaks the four-corp partition
  • Distributes combat across all four — dilutes every role's core mechanic

Reference points: Factorio uses combat as backdrop pressure that taxes expansion (still a factory game in essence). Mindustry is a tower defense first, factory second. Anno keeps combat optional. Captain of Industry has none. The cleanest factory tycoons treat combat as deliberately minimal or absent.

Soft alternatives that capture "external threat" without becoming an RTS
MechanicWhat it addsGenre fit
Corporate espionageNPC corps plant moles, leak recipes, sabotage supply; counter with counterintel spendCiv-style; very cyberpunk
Hostile takeoversNPC corps buy out underperforming players' assets; business defendsTycoon-native
Regulatory pressureGovernment enforcement, environmental fines, labor strikesEvent-driven, no combat
Market warfareNPC price wars, predatory pricing, dumping campaignsPure economic conflict
Disasters & eventsPower outages, pipeline failures, weather, agri disease"System under attack" feel without combat units

These give the kinetic-energy feel without changing the genre. Several pair naturally with the narrative event system in 7.3 — espionage, regulatory pressure, and takeovers can all be modeled as event chains.

Recommendation: no to literal combat. Worth picking 1–2 of the soft alternatives above as v1 external-pressure mechanics. The cyberpunk theme makes corporate espionage and regulatory/political pressure particularly on-brand.

7.3 Narrative event system (Ambrus's proposal)

Ambrus proposed adding narrative decisions in the style of Humankind or Endless Space 2: branching event chains, some shared and group-decided, some corp-exclusive. This is a strong addition that earns its own design pillar.

How the three event types work

  • Shared events — group-decision moments. "The local government is offering tax breaks if we host a regional trade fair — costs us 30 days of warehouse capacity. Yes / no?" Exactly the kind of conversation that makes co-op feel alive.
  • Corp-exclusive events — private agency moments and mini-arcs. "As the agri corp, you've been offered an exclusive deal with a heritage seed bank — take it and your future yields shift toward diversity over volume." Reinforces corp identity and feeds emergent sub-type formation (7.1).
  • Cross-corp events (affecting two of four) — deepen pairwise dynamics. "Industrial expansion has triggered water-shortage rumors; business can issue a press denial ($$$) or admit and propose remediation."
Architectural insight — events feed the business corp's order/contract system

A key synthesis: events shouldn't be a parallel system to the business corp's orders & contracts mechanic — they should generate orders. Many events naturally take the form of an external party offering or demanding a production deliverable:

  • "A new mega-mall in the next district has placed an order for 5,000 units of premium beer in 45 days — accept?"  → business takes the contract; industrial scrambles to retool; agri ramps barley; logistics reshapes routes
  • "Government wants 2,000 units of agri-grade emergency rations for a regional crisis — discounted price but reputation-positive"
  • "Black-market broker wants 500 units of an unlicensed product — high pay, regulatory risk"

This means D-11 (external pressure), D-12 (events), and the business corp's existing order-handling are one coupled system, not three. The "guys, can we do this?" moment becomes a single design surface, with events as one of the order sources alongside ordinary NPC market demand.

Design note: events that turn into contract obligations need a clear negotiation step — business shouldn't be able to unilaterally accept an order that the production corps can't deliver. The right shape is "business proposes, the table agrees, then the contract is signed" — same rhythm as a research vote on the shared tech tree.

Why this fits the project

  • Adds personality and story without authored campaign content
  • Gives players a reason to care about the world beyond pure optimization
  • Creates memorable session moments — the "remember when..." stories that make a game live in players' heads after the session ends
  • Pairs perfectly with cyberpunk theme — corporate scandals, government negotiations, hostile media, whistleblowers
  • Architecturally light: each event is trigger + presentation + effect. Data-driven content. Ship a basic system, grow the event library over time
  • Naturally absorbs the soft external-threat mechanics from 7.2 — espionage, regulatory pressure, takeovers can all be implemented as event chains rather than dedicated systems
  • Fits cleanly with corp sub-types from 7.1 — each sub-type can carry its own private event arcs, multiplying replayability further

Design considerations

  • Events queue and fire at safe moments (between orders, between days) to avoid mid-crunch interruption
  • Time-pressured events display visible countdowns; non-urgent events offer "consult later"
  • v1 library scope: ~8–10 shared events + ~4–5 corp-exclusive per corp = ~30 events for a credible launch. Subsequent events become natural update / DLC content
  • A small writer/designer pass at the right time would significantly elevate the system; could be a place where Ambrus contributes beyond visuals

Reference systems worth studying

  • Endless Space 2 — minor-faction quests, hero arcs, event chains tied to galactic state
  • Stellaris — anomaly + event-chain system, very mature design; the gold standard for data-driven 4X events
  • Humankind — civic decisions and narrative beats
  • Crusader Kings 3 — character-driven event chains; good model for corp-exclusive arcs
  • Frostpunk — moral-cost decisions under resource pressure; very on-brand for cyberpunk corporate tycoon

Recommendation: strong yes. This feature gives the game character and depth disproportionate to its engineering cost, and it absorbs / enables several other design wishes — external pressure (7.2), corp identity, and replayability when paired with sub-types (7.1). It is a v1 design pillar, not a "nice to have."

7.4 The replayability flywheel — how the three threads interlock

Pulling the three threads together: replayability in Drinkustry comes from the interaction of three coupled systems, not from picking from a longer list of corps at session start.

The three-system flywheel
  1. Shared tech tree (§5) — forces strategic conversation about pacing and direction. What we unlock together.
  2. Corp-exclusive tech tree / upgrade branches (§7.1) — drives emergent sub-type identity per corp. How my corp specialises this run.
  3. Narrative events (§7.3, coupled with business orders / external pressure) — creates session-defining moments and reactive play. What happens to us this run.

Each session writes its own story along all three axes simultaneously. Same role twice still gives different runs. No setup-screen choice paralysis. And every choice the player makes — what to research with shared credits, what to upgrade in their corp tree, how to react to events — becomes identity-defining rather than just optimisation.

This resolves Gergely's "more corps" question. The answer to "how do we get more variety?" isn't more roles or more pickable factions — it's more depth on the three axes above. That's where the design effort goes.

D-13 · Session shape — three game modes [provisionally agreed]
Status: provisionally agreed · Refs: §9 "Game modes" callout

Outcome: ship three modes that complement each other — custom maps / sandbox with full parameter tuning (map size, economic difficulty, resource scarcity, NPC hostility, event frequency), predefined scenarios with fixed settings and tech-level constraints, and a campaign mode sequencing scenarios with escalating difficulty plus persistent unlocks/bonuses between them.

This resolves the original session-length tension: groups can pick a sandbox parameter set that matches the time they have, scenarios deliver tight 1–1.5 hr experiences for short windows, and campaigns reward committed friend groups across multiple sessions. Open implementation questions are split into D-14 (build order) and D-15 (persistent unlock model).

D-14 · Game mode build order & v1 scope
Owner: both · Effort: scoping · Refs: §9 "Game modes" callout

The three modes have very different content costs:

  • Sandbox / custom maps — cheapest to ship; players generate variety from tuned parameters. Right v1 mode.
  • Scenarios — mid-cost; each scenario is hand-designed content, balanced against an expected starting position. Good update / DLC pipeline.
  • Campaign — highest cost; sequence design + cross-scenario progression + narrative connective tissue. Strong marketing hook but real production effort.

Recommendation: ship sandbox as v1; scenarios as post-launch content updates; campaign for a major update or v2. Locks in the right scope for a small team without closing any doors.

D-15 · Persistent unlock model — team, per-corp, or both?
Owner: both · Effort: design · Refs: §9 "Game modes" callout

If campaign progression unlocks techs/bonuses between scenarios, who owns the unlocks?

  • Team unlocks — the four-friend table accumulates permanent bonuses together. Reinforces co-op-essential commitment; encourages the same group to play through the campaign together.
  • Per-corp unlocks — each player's corp tree gets new permanent options. Rewards specialisation and matches the emergent-sub-types direction (D-10).
  • Both — most interesting design but most complex and largest balance surface.

Decision shapes how campaign mode interacts with the corp identity system. Worth deferring until D-10 (corp tech tree) is more concrete.

D-16 · AI failsafe for disconnects / no-shows
Owner: Gergely · Effort: scoping · Phase 12+

Confirming the four-player co-op-only direction unlocks deeper mechanic design. As a pragmatic safety net (not as a real SP mode), include an AI failsafe that takes over a disconnected or no-show corp to keep the session playable. Scope is deliberately small:

  • Keeps the corp's existing operations running (factories don't stop, deliveries continue)
  • Makes sane, non-optimal decisions — accept reasonable contracts, don't block the team, don't take risky bets
  • Does not negotiate — for shared tech-tree votes or contract acceptance, defaults to "abstain" or "follow majority"
  • Visibly flagged in UI ("Agri Corp · AI failsafe — Pete reconnecting") so the table knows it's a placeholder, not a real partner

This is meaningfully smaller than a "real AI corp partner" — the hardest AI problem (negotiation) is sidestepped. Natural to scope into Phase 12 (networked MP) since disconnect handling lives there anyway. Architectural bonus: if designed with reuse in mind, the same failsafe code could later support a "solo training mode" for onboarding (one human, three scripted partners) — same engine, two purposes.

Playtest plan

A real answer to "who plays a co-op-only game during dev?":

  • Phase 10–11 (private playtest): existing close-knit friend group that already plays games together regularly. Sessions scheduled ahead of time. Trusted, available, no recruiting overhead.
  • Phase 12+ (early access / alpha-beta): grow a Discord community as the playtest pool, similar to how Deep Rock Galactic / Risk of Rain 2 / Valheim run their pipelines. Crossed when we get there; not over-engineering it now.

Session length is handled by the three-mode design below — groups can pick the mode that matches the time they have.

Game modes — three-mode shipping shape

Drinkustry ships three complementary modes that together cover short, long, and infinite play patterns:

  • Sandbox / custom maps — full parameter tuning before sit-down: map size, economic difficulty, resource scarcity, NPC corp hostility, event frequency, starting tech level, etc. The table picks parameters to match the time they have. Infinite-replayability hook. Cheapest to ship; right primary mode for v1.
  • Predefined scenarios — fixed settings, tech-level constraints, specific objectives, hand-designed for ~1–1.5 hr arcs. Polished-experience hook; great for first plays, demos, marketing trailers, streaming. Mid-cost — each scenario is content. Good update / DLC pipeline.
  • Campaign mode — sequence of scenarios with escalating difficulty, persistent unlocks/bonuses carried between them. Story-driven progression hook — the most marketable of the three modes, but the highest-cost to produce (sequence design, cross-scenario progression, narrative connective tissue). Reserve for a major update or v2.

Genre pattern: Frostpunk, Captain of Industry, Anno 1800, AoE2, Northgard all ship some configuration of this trio. Drinkustry should follow the same pattern. See D-14 for build-order recommendation and D-15 for persistent unlock design.

Two design notes worth flagging now
  • Tunable parameters have a hidden content cost. Every knob you expose needs to be playtested at extremes, balanced against the others, and clearly explained in UI. Captain of Industry exposes many; Anno 1800 is more curated. Decide intentionally how exposed-vs-curated the sandbox settings are — too few feels shallow, too many overwhelms first-time players.
  • Campaign needs narrative connective tissue, not just rising difficulty. Stacking scenarios that get harder is fine but unmemorable. A campaign that has even minimal story progression — a megacorp arc unfolding scenario by scenario — feels like a real game mode, not "harder versions of the same level." This is exactly where the narrative event system from §7.3 pays off doubly: each campaign scenario can carry its own bespoke event chain. The campaign mode is the place where Drinkustry's writing voice can really land.

10. Art Track — Parallel Workstream

Ambrus's stated preference (2026-05-01): concrete asset production for buildings and UI elements waits until mechanics are decided and tested. Drawing what we don't yet know means redoing work when mechanics shift. The art track is therefore split into two tiers — work that runs in parallel with mechanic design, and work that's gated on mechanic lock.

Tier 1 — runs in parallel (informs mechanic discussions, doesn't depend on locked buildings)
  1. Cyberpunk reference compilation — a curated PureRef sub-board pulling from Mindustry, Cyberpunk 2077 corporate scenes, Deus Ex UI, megacorp branding from real-world references. Cheap, useful as conversation fuel for design discussions.
  2. Four corp visual languages — one-pager per corp: logo system, color palette, building silhouette language, signature material, key motif. Designed in isolation but tested together. This work informs the corp identity discussion (D-10) — having visual language helps us think about what a "BioCorp" run feels like vs. a "Heritage" run.
    • Agri: organic / biotech / verdant — engineered nature
    • Industrial: brutalist / heavy / smoking — power and mass
    • Logistics: sleek / networked / kinetic — flow and connection
    • Business: sleek / corporate / luxurious — boardrooms and brand
  3. One painted "key art" scene — a single illustration that establishes the world's tone. Useful for marketing, pitch, and as a reference touchstone. Doesn't depend on building lists.
Tier 2 — gated on mechanic lock (deferred per Ambrus 2026-05-01)
  • Concrete building sprites and machine icons — wait until the building list, machine list, and production chains are locked through playtest. Producing these against an unstable spec means redoing them.
  • Final UI element design (HUD, menus, panels, the actual tech-tree screen) — wait until the underlying systems are stable enough that the UI is being designed against real interactions, not hypothetical ones.
  • Tech-tree boardroom UI mock — useful as a tier-1 mood/concept mock at low fidelity (loose sketch establishing the boardroom feel), but final UI work waits until the tech tree's data model is locked.

The pattern: low-fidelity sketches in service of design conversations are fine and welcome at any phase; final asset production waits for mechanic lock. Even tier-2 work can have a paper-prototype version that informs mechanic discussions without committing the artist's time to anything that might be thrown away. This keeps art's voice in the design conversation without paying redo costs.

11. Comparative Reference — Anno & Sierra Historical City Builders

The 2026-05-01 design pass surfaced two reference families worth analyzing explicitly: Anno (1404, 1800, 2070, 2205, 117) and the Sierra / Impressions historical city builders (Caesar III, Pharaoh + Cleopatra, Zeus: Master of Olympus + Poseidon, Emperor: Rise of the Middle Kingdom). Both franchises sit upstream of the modern production-chain tycoon genre, and several Drinkustry mechanics are inheriting their conventions implicitly. Worth pulling them onto the table.

11.1 Shared DNA — what we are already doing that they pioneered

Drinkustry's chain shape is closest to Anno's. Its suitability handling is closest to Pharaoh's floodplain logic. Its storage role and demand-sink shape are shared across both franchises. We are not stealing — we are extending an inherited tradition into asymmetric co-op territory.

11.2 Mechanics worth borrowing — concrete proposals

Anno settlement tiers → city demand evolution

Anno's defining loop: a population tier demands specific goods; satisfying demand consistently lets the tier evolve, which unlocks the next tier and demands more diverse goods.

Drinkustry analogue: cities, towns, and outposts each have a tier (e.g., Outpost → Town → City → Megacity). At the lowest tier they demand only lager beer; consistent supply for a duration evolves them, and they begin demanding cider alongside; further evolution adds spirits, premium products, branded variants. This gives the business corp a visible map of growth and a structural reason for the production corps to keep diversifying. Pairs cleanly with the existing pollution-shrinks-suitable-land mechanic — late game has fewer fields but richer markets, forcing optimisation.

Architectural fit: this dovetails with the 2026-05-01 gameplay doc's "demand isn't a problem early game; market shifts later" framing. Settlement tiers are the explicit mechanism behind that shift.

Sierra monuments → the Co-op Megaproject

Pyramids in Pharaoh, the Parthenon in Zeus, the Great Wall in Emperor — multi-year construction projects requiring sustained logistics from many industries simultaneously. They are the city's shared pinnacle.

Drinkustry analogue: a slow-build endgame megaproject the four corps pursue together. Candidate forms:

  • Megaplant — one giant brewery requiring constant raw input from agri, custom machinery from industrial, dedicated trunk lines from logistics, and pre-sold contracts from business
  • Flagship Brand — multi-stage marketing campaign that elevates the table's reputation and unlocks new market tiers
  • District Reclamation — reverse a polluted region, requires the fertilizer-loop sustained over time, returns valuable suitable land

Gives campaigns and scenarios a natural climactic objective without scripted authoring. The "guys, can we do this together?" question scales from per-contract to per-megaproject.

Anno specialists / items → corp specialists
Anno 1800's items are equippable buffs slotted into buildings: yield boosts, throughput speedups, recipe changers. Drinkustry analogue: the per-corp internal tech tree (§5, §7.4) could deliver part of its bonuses through equippable specialists — "Master Maltster" assigned to a malthouse, "Hops Whisperer" assigned to an agri field. Gives the corp-internal tree texture beyond pure stat tweaks, and gives players visible decisions about where their corp's progress is being spent. Naturally absorbs corp-exclusive event rewards (§7.3) — events can hand out named specialists.
Sierra festivals → city-targeted ad campaigns
Sierra's festivals are scheduled, paid events that generate temporary city-wide bonuses (or appease angry deities). Drinkustry analogue: the business corp's ad campaign mechanic (§4) sharpens into festivals — scheduled, paid, city-targeted events that spike demand or accelerate tier evolution at a chosen settlement. Reads more grounded than a generic "ad campaign" and creates a natural "pick the city we'll push this run" moment. Pairs with the settlement-tier system above.
Anno influence → reputation as a meta-currency
Anno 1800's influence is a meta-resource that gates diplomacy, propaganda, and expedition power — parallel to credits. Drinkustry analogue: a reputation meta-currency tracked per corp and per table. Spent on accepting better contracts, running festivals, navigating regulatory pressure (D-11). Lost on accepting black-market deals or letting pollution run wild. Earned through finished contracts, festival success, environmental remediation. Adds a third resource axis (alongside materials and credits) that the four corps must coordinate on, and gives the business corp a non-monetary lever to manage.
Pharaoh's seasonal cycles → harvest seasons
Pharaoh's calendar centers on the annual Nile flood: a forced rhythm that shapes every farming decision. Drinkustry analogue: certain crops carry seasonal cycles — apples have a harvest window, hops have a flowering month, future grapes have a vintage year. Adds a temporal beat to agri's verb set and gives long-aging spirits (whisky, calvados) a natural mechanical home. Optional in slice 1; appears alongside orchards as a slice-2+ feature.

11.3 Mechanics deliberately not carried over

Sierra walker AI — too granular for our logistics scope
Walker-based logistics (each worker walks the road carrying one good) is iconic but it would micro-shred Drinkustry's logistics corp into per-tile pathfinding management, against an abstracted vehicle-route model that's already working at Phase 7B. Worth admiring as an inspiration, not as an implementation.
Sierra housing evolution — we don't simulate citizens
In Sierra builders, every house is a unit that evolves through housing tiers as services arrive. Drinkustry's "settlements as demand sinks" approach is much lighter — cities are entities with tiers, not collections of simulated households. The Anno settlement-tier mechanic above (§11.2) gives us the same gameplay shape without the simulation cost.
Anno expeditions — out of scope for v1
Anno 1800's expedition system (send a ship with crew/supplies/skills to resolve away missions) is great but cuts against the four-corp negotiation focus. Saved as a v2+ idea if a "trade expedition" fits into the business corp's late game.
Sierra mythology / gods → reskinned as cyberpunk regulatory bodies
Zeus's gods, Pharaoh's pantheon — divine actors who reward festivals and punish neglect. Doesn't fit a cyberpunk megacorp setting literally, but the shape reskins cleanly: government regulators, media bodies, AI overlords as the "deities" we appease through compliance, ad spend, and donations. This bends back into the narrative event system (§7.3) rather than becoming its own subsystem.
Anno combat / Sierra military — already declined (D-11)
Anno is combat-optional; Sierra has tower-defense-style invasions in some campaigns. Drinkustry explicitly opted out of literal combat in §7.2 / D-11. The soft alternatives (espionage, regulatory pressure, market warfare) cover the same "external threat" beat without changing the genre.

11.4 Where Drinkustry diverges

Three commitments that Anno and Sierra do not share, and that genuinely change the design problem:

  • Asymmetric four-player co-op. Anno and Sierra are SP. Drinkustry's whole spine — shared tech tree, cross-corp negotiation, contract acceptance as a table decision — only exists because of co-op. The "alpha player" failure mode (§6) is a problem those games never had to solve.
  • Inside-the-building factory layout. Anno's production buildings are black boxes; Sierra's are too. Drinkustry's industrial corp steps inside to design machine layouts Mindustry / Modulus-style. This is the depth axis those references don't have.
  • Cyberpunk megacorp framing. Anno reskins across eras; Sierra reskins across civilizations. Drinkustry's cyberpunk reskin is closer to Anno 2070 than to anything Sierra shipped — but the narrative pillar (events as corporate scandals, regulator inspections, black-market deals) leans heavier than Anno did.
One-line positioning
Drinkustry = Anno's production chains and tier demand + Sierra's industry-specific suitability and shared-monument feel + Mindustry's inside-the-building factory + cyberpunk megacorp reskin + asymmetric four-player co-op. The first two ingredients are decades-tested; the last three are where the design's originality lives.

11.5 What this analysis implies for the open decisions

  • D-11 (external pressure) — Sierra-style "deity" appeasement reframed as regulatory bodies fits the cyberpunk theme cleanly. Picks up festivals as a natural sub-mechanic.
  • D-12 (narrative events) — settlement-tier evolution and reputation meta-currency are structural pillars the event library can hook into; events become more meaningful when they shift tier or reputation.
  • D-13/D-14 (game modes) — the Co-op Megaproject is a natural scenario objective and a natural campaign climax. Strengthens the case for scenarios as a content surface.
  • D-15 (persistent unlocks) — Anno's specialists/items map onto per-corp persistent unlocks cleanly: the campaign hands out named specialists across scenarios.
  • 2026-05-01 gameplay doc Q3 — settlement tiers give business a clear spatial purpose: place outlets, evolve cities, choose which markets to push. Resolves "is business just a UI layer?" in favour of strong spatial presence.
