1. Purpose & Method

The cider doc flagged aging as a possible variety axis ("timer change") and the wine entry parked aging as "the entire game later." This doc pulls aging out as its own design surface because:

  • Half the drinks the project will eventually want involve real aging (lagering, barrel-aged stout, traditional cider, wine, all spirits).
  • Aging is the natural source of a time-shift / speculation mechanic — the kind of meta-decision a tycoon game lives or dies on.
  • It touches all four corps simultaneously, so it doesn't fit cleanly inside any single chain doc.
  • It pairs naturally with narrative events (fire, regulation, market crash) — multiplying the value of both.
The driving question
If the business player can choose to hold a batch instead of selling it, and the value of that held batch changes over time differently for every drink — what does that decision feel like, who else is involved in making it, and what fails if we don't get it right?

Working assumption

Aging is not v1 for the lager slice. It probably arrives somewhere between cider (slice 2) and wine (slice 3+), as the second drink makes "which to ship now / which to hold" a meaningful question. The lager slice should leave hooks for it; this doc is what those hooks should anticipate.

2. Real-World Aging — What's Actually True

A reality check before designing on top. Quick reference of which drinks actually benefit from holding.

DrinkAging in real lifeGame implication
Lager / Pilsner "Lagering" — cold conditioning, weeks to months. Mandatory part of the style. Short, required aging step; not really a speculation lever — built into the chain timer.
Standard ale / IPA Best fresh. IPA hop aroma actively fades over months. IPA's aging curve is negative — speculative holding punishes the player. Strong asymmetry.
Porter / Stout Standard versions modest benefit. Imperial stouts age beautifully. Two sub-curves on the same chain — design hook for "regular vs imperial."
Sour / Lambic Open fermentation, long aging in oak — months to years. Best aging curve in beer. Pairs with the event-driven flag from the cider doc.
Modern industrial cider Sold fresh, like commodity beer. Flat aging curve. Sell now.
Traditional / barrel-aged cider Months in barrel or bottle. Ice cider, keeved cider, whisky-barrel cider all real. Strong positive curve. Premium product tier.
Wine Aging is the entire identity of the product — months to decades. Aging is the chain. Wine should not ship before the aging system does.
Spirits (whiskey, brandy, calvados) Legally cannot be sold under minimum aging in most categories. Aging is mandatory output gate, not optional speculation.
Three flavors of aging, mechanically
Real-world aging maps to three game roles: (1) required maturation built into the chain timer (lagering, spirit minimum aging); (2) optional speculation on a positive curve (barrel cider, imperial stout, sours); (3) negative aging that punishes holding (IPA, fresh ales). All three should exist — the asymmetry is the design.

3. The Core Loop — Sell Now, or Hold for the Curve

The base mechanic is small. A finished batch arrives at a junction: it can route to a sales outlet (sells now at current market price) or to a storage building (sits in capacity until released). Released batches carry a value modifier from their aging curve, which the sales outlet honors.

Step 1 · Industrial
Batch finishes brewing → arrives at building output bay
Step 2 · Logistics
Truck routes the batch — destination chosen via a per-batch flag set by business: sell now or send to storage
Step 3a · Sell
Delivered to sales outlet at current price. Done.
Step 3b · Hold
Delivered to cellar / warehouse / barrel store. Occupies a slot. Aging timer starts; value modifier rises (or falls, for IPA-style) along the drink's curve.
Step 4 · Release
Business decides when to release. Logistics picks up from storage → sales outlet at the modified price.

The decision the business player is actually making

  • Read the aging curve for this drink — when does the value plateau? When does it peak?
  • Read the market forecast — will demand be higher next season? Lower?
  • Read the storage budget — is the cellar full? Is logistics squeezed by harvest pulses elsewhere?
  • Read the risk — has the events feed flagged anything that could cost the held inventory?

This is the first mechanic where the business player has a real, recurring, spatial decision that isn't "place an outlet" — and it's a decision logistics has to honor and industrial has to provision for.

Why this is small and big at the same time
Mechanically: one new building family, one new per-batch flag, one curve table. Strategically: it changes how every other system gets played, because now "we made too much" isn't necessarily a problem and "demand is low this season" isn't necessarily a crisis.

4. Aging Curves Per Drink Type

Five curve archetypes. Every drink in the project should slot into one of these, possibly with parameter tweaks. Real curves can be tuned later; the archetypes are the design vocabulary.

Flat

Modern cider, standard ale. No reason to hold.

Lager, pilsner, all spirits. Cannot ship before the gate. Built into chain timer, not a player choice.

Slow positive

Barrel cider, porter, standard stout. Modest premium for holding through the curve. Plateaus.

Steep positive

Long-Aged Premium

value 4 ╭── │ ╱ 2 ╭─╯ │ ╱ 1 ──╯ 0 └──────────► time

Imperial stout, lambic, wine, aged spirits. High ceiling, long horizon. Storage capacity is the real cost.

Negative

Stale Punishment

value 1 ──╮ │ ╲ │ ╲___ 0 └──────────► time

IPA, fresh ales. Holding loses money. Forces business to actually decide instead of always-hold.

Peak then decay

Drink-By Window

value 2 ╭╮ │ ╱ ╲ 1 ──╯ ╲___ 0 └──────────► time

Some traditional ciders, certain wines past peak. Hold too long and the gain reverses — strongest skill expression.

Curve archetypes shouldn't be hidden information. The business player should see the curve for any drink they're considering — the skill is reading the market and storage situation against a known curve, not memorising hidden values.

5. Storage Buildings & Spatial Layer

Aging is spatial — it occupies finite slots in physical buildings on the map. This is what gives logistics a stake (capacity routing) and grounds the speculation in something the players can see.

Building family SEED

BuildingCapacityCurve modifierUtility needsNotes
Cold cellar Medium Suits short-required & flat-positive Power (refrigeration) The lager-conditioning building. Cheap, efficient, low ceiling.
Barrel store Small per slot, oak barrels Boost steep-positive curves Climate-stable, low power The stout / sour / spirit aging building. Barrels themselves may be a consumable resource Q.
Bottle warehouse Large Flat or slow-positive only Minimal Bulk holding for finished, packaged inventory. The "hold for next season" building.
Wine cellar (later) Medium Boost peak-then-decay; long timers Climate-stable Wine-doc territory. Listed for completeness.

What this gives logistics

  • Routing decisions per batch — output bay → sales outlet vs output bay → storage building.
  • Capacity tetris — storage buildings are finite; full storage means batches back up at the brewery.
  • A reason to expand the network — multiple storage buildings, dedicated routes between them.

What this gives industrial

  • A new building family that isn't a brewery — broadens the corp's spatial vocabulary.
  • Power demand spike (cold cellars, refrigerated warehouses) — couples to logistics' power network.
  • (Later) barrel production as its own minor industrial chain — wood input, cooperage building, barrels feed storage. Optional depth knob.
The "barrel as consumable" question
Real barrels age out (the wood imparts less character with each fill). Treating barrels as a slowly-degrading consumable creates a nice secondary loop: agri grows wood → industrial cooperage builds barrels → barrels populate barrel stores → barrels eventually deplete and need replacing. Probably too much for slice 2/3. Park as a slice-N depth lever once base aging is shipped.

6. What Each Corp Does in the Aging Layer

The reason this doc exists as its own page: every corp does something here. Aging isn't a business-corp feature with logistics overhead; it's a four-corp interaction.

Corp 01 · Agricultural

Indirect, but felt.

  • Held inventory means industrial draws less from the silo this season — agri's offtake becomes lumpy.
  • Specialty inputs (oats, special hops, specific apple cultivars) become more valuable when they feed long-aging premium drinks. Agri's planting plan shifts.
  • (Later) Wood/forest plantations feed cooperage if barrels become consumable.
Corp 02 · Industrial

Builds the storage. Decides what to make for aging.

  • Places cellars, barrel stores, warehouses on the strategic map. New building silhouettes.
  • Brewery output schedule no longer "brew → ship" — now "brew → tag for hold or sell."
  • Recipe choices start to interact with aging: brewing imperial stout only makes sense if storage is ready.
  • Power demand grows for refrigerated storage. Couples to logistics' power network.
Corp 03 · Logistics

Routes batches. Manages capacity tetris.

  • Per-batch routing to outlet or storage. The route node graph carries an extra flag per cargo.
  • Storage capacity is finite — when full, batches back up. Logistics has to negotiate with industrial about what to age.
  • Surge planning: a held batch released for sale needs trucks now, on top of normal flow.
Corp 04 · Economic / Marketing

The corp this mechanic was built for.

  • Sets the per-batch flag — sell now, or send to storage.
  • Reads the aging curves and the market forecast; decides release timing per held batch.
  • Aged inventory can be marketed as a premium SKU at a different sales outlet ("Reserve" / "Cask" / "Aged X"). Direct hook to ad-campaign and brand-building features.
  • Speculation on bulk orders: "Government wants 200 crates of 3-year stout in two seasons" — a contract that requires committing storage now.
  • Inter-player loans take on new meaning: "lend me credits, my warehouse releases in 2 seasons."
Co-op pressure points this creates
  • Industrial vs Business: "stop brewing imperial stout, the warehouse is full" — real disagreement, real conversation.
  • Logistics vs Business: "every truck I have is hauling held inventory you're releasing" — surge events.
  • Agri vs Business: "you contracted me to grow specialty oats but you're sitting on the stout" — supply pacing.
  • All vs events: "the warehouse just caught fire and three years of stout is gone" — collective loss, collective decisions about insurance / redundancy.

7. Risk, Spoilage & Event Hooks

Holding inventory must carry risk, or the optimal play is "always hold everything that has a positive curve." Risk is what makes the speculation a real decision.

Continuous risk (always present)

  • Spoilage — small ongoing chance a slot loses its batch. Higher in poorly-maintained storage; lower in proper cellars.
  • Opportunity cost — credits tied up in held inventory aren't earning. The business player feels this implicitly through cashflow.
  • Capacity cost — every full slot is a slot the next batch can't use.

Event-driven risk (narrative events) SEED

  • Fire / disaster — full or partial loss of a storage building. Higher stakes the longer the held curves.
  • Regulatory seizure — government fault flagging a batch (contamination claim, tax dispute) for a season. Pairs with the regulatory-pressure pillar.
  • Market crash — demand for premium aged products drops; the curve flattens or inverts mid-hold.
  • Espionage — rival corp action contaminates or leaks a held batch. Cyberpunk-flavored sabotage hook.
  • Boom — the inverse: regional event spikes demand for aged stock specifically. Players who held look prescient; players who sold early look unlucky.
Risk has to bite, but not too hard
If aging is "free with one rare disaster," everyone always ages. If aging is "high disaster rate," nobody ages. The tuning sweet spot is somewhere players feel ~5–15% of held value evaporate to risk over a long campaign — present enough to weigh, not so much it dominates. Numbers are speculative; this needs playtest.

8. Why This Belongs in v1+ (and What It Unlocks)

Not v1 (the lager slice doesn't need it). But the project should commit to it as the next-tier strategic layer, because it unlocks several things at once:

  • Business gets a real decision loop. Without aging, business is "place an outlet, set the income split, watch credits arrive." With aging, business is reading curves, making timing calls, negotiating with logistics — exactly the active spatial role the 2026-04-30 doc said this corp needs.
  • Wine and spirits become possible to build. Both depend entirely on aging. The infrastructure shipped here is the infrastructure those drinks need.
  • Events become higher-stakes. A fire that destroys a half-grown barley field is annoying. A fire that destroys 3 years of imperial stout is a campaign moment.
  • Corp sub-types get real anchors. "Heritage Co-op" agri, "Luxury Brand" business, "Refinery Specialist" industrial — these emergent identities need actual mechanics that reward the specialty. Aging is one of the strongest such mechanics: a player committed to aged premium products plays differently from a mass-market player.
  • The shared tech tree gets weight. Storage tier upgrades, climate-control tech, barrel quality, market-forecast accuracy — natural shared-tree branches that all four corps care about.
The bigger frame
Aging isn't a feature; it's the strategic layer the project has been pointing at without naming. The 2026-04-30 doc said business needs spatial presence. The 2026-05-02 memo said "menu player risk" for business. The events memo said events need stakes. Aging answers all three at once.

9. Open Questions

Q1. Per-batch granularity, or per-route default?
Per-batch: business flags every batch individually — high control, high UI cost. Per-route default: brewery output route is "60% to outlet, 40% to cellar" — lower control, lower UI cost. Hybrid is probably the answer (default split + override per batch).
Q2. Are aging curves visible to the player, or hidden?
Lean: visible. The skill is reading the market against a known curve, not memorising hidden numbers. Hidden curves penalize new players and don't reward veterans meaningfully more.
Q3. Is storage strictly capacity-limited, or are there diminishing returns?
Strict capacity is honest and simple. Diminishing returns ("a fourth full warehouse provides 70% benefit") could prevent late-game runaway dominance, but it's a softer system to read.
Q4. Should aged batches occupy a different SKU at sales outlets?
Mechanically simple: same drink, value modifier. Visually richer: outlet shows "Aged Stout — Reserve" vs "Stout." The richer version directly hooks into ad campaigns and luxury branding. Lean: ship simple first, add SKU split when ad campaigns come online.
Q5. Insurance as a business-corp mechanic against storage events?
A natural fit: business pays a premium per slot, gets payout on disaster. Adds a strategic knob, fits the financial-corp identity. Could be a tech-tree unlock.
Q6. Can aged batches be traded between players, or only sold via outlets?
In co-op, "all four players already share credits-ish" so this is mostly a UI question. In future competitive mode, aged batches as tradeable commodities is a serious feature.
Q7. How visible is the speculation to the other corps?
If business holds 200 crates of stout silently, the other corps just see "industrial output rising, sales income flat" without knowing why. Some transparency UI ("aging dashboard" available to all corps) keeps the table on the same page.
Q8. Does aging interact with the pollution map?
Probably no for v1+; let aging be a clean economic layer without entangling the heat-map system. But "nearby pollution accelerates spoilage" is a flavorful seed for later.
Q9. Which slice introduces the first real aged drink?
Options: porter (familiar, modest curve), barrel cider (hooks the cider chain into aging immediately), imperial stout (most dramatic curve). Lean: porter first as the gentle introduction; barrel cider second to extend the cider chain; imperial stout once players have learned to read curves.
Q10. Barrels — abstract ("storage slot") or concrete (consumable resource produced by cooperage)?
Abstract for v1 of aging; concrete much later as a depth lever. Concrete barrels would unlock real corp sub-type identities (barrel-focused agri-forestry, barrel-quality industrial niche).
Q11. Aging curve tuning — real-time scaled, or based on harvest cycles?
Echoes the orchard maturation question from the cider doc. Same answer is probably correct: scale to harvest cycles, not wall-clock, so a one-hour session can still feel a meaningful aging arc.
Q12. UI — where does the business player actually see all this?
Probably a dedicated panel: list of held batches, aging progress, projected release value, current curve position. Inspirations: stock-portfolio screens, but make it feel like a tycoon ledger, not a financial app.

10. Scratch / Parking Lot

Half-formed thoughts
  • Cyberpunk reskin: "cellars" become climate-controlled bunkers; "barrels" become pressurized aging tanks. Same mechanics, sleeker silhouettes.
  • Premium-aged products could feed a separate luxury sales outlet tier — different demand region, different clientele, fits "Luxury Brand" business sub-type.
  • Aging-related achievement / vanity trigger when a single batch is held longer than X cycles and survives — gives the table a "we did it" beat at release time.
  • The aging mechanic is the cleanest answer to "what does business do for the entire mid-game" — until aging exists, business runs out of decisions after slice 1.
  • Held inventory as collateral for loans — business can borrow against the projected release value. Risky but flavorful.
  • Heritage / period varieties: a flag on a batch that says "this was held during [event X]" — the event becomes part of the SKU's marketing story. Pairs with the events memo.
  • Cross-corp signal idea: when business marks a batch for long-aging, that signal could propagate to agri ("your specialty oats fed a 3-year batch") for a small reputation/morale beat. Tiny, but it makes the table feel connected.
  • For competitive mode much later: cellar raids / storage espionage as a real action verb. Park.
