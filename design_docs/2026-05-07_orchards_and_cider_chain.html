1. Purpose & Method

The 2026-05-01 doc nailed the lager chain end-to-end. This doc adds the second chain — cider — and the agri building it requires: orchards. The point isn't to invent new mechanics. It's to prove the chain template generalises, and to introduce a long-cycle commitment to the agri player without changing the existing systems (heat map, water, pollution, manual tending).

The driving question
What is the smallest set of additions on top of the lager slice that gives the four corps a second drink to make, with a different rhythm — and what does that teach each player about the system?

Working assumptions carried forward

  • Existing system principles from 2026-05-01 (water + power, agri-input-per-recipe, biological + sewage waste, pollution heat map, two-layer tech, machine reuse where chemistry overlaps) all apply unchanged.
  • Per-chain industrial buildings rather than hot-swap (Q5 lean from 2026-05-01) — cider gets its own brewery building.
  • Silo catchment-radius pickup model (per 2026-05-02 per-corp v1) applies to fruit silos identically.

Tag legend

  • SET — design call made; carried into the slice unless re-opened.
  • SEED — starting idea worth challenging.
  • TBD — explicitly empty; needs a call.
  • Q — open question for the next session.

2. Orchards vs. Crop Fields — the Asymmetry

Orchards reuse every existing system (suitability, water, pollution, manual tending) but on a different timescale and with a different commitment shape. The point of orchards is not new mechanics — it's that the same mechanics produce a fundamentally different feel for the agri player.

Crop fields (existing)

  • 1×1 footprint, generic farmland tile
  • Annual cycle — replant each season
  • Crop swap = free between cycles
  • Tending pressure: continuous (water, nutrients, pH every cycle)
  • Yield: steady trickle per cycle
  • Pollution loss: one cycle's harvest — replant elsewhere
  • Investment payback: fast

Orchards (new)

  • 2×2 (or larger) footprint — readable from above
  • Multi-cycle perennial — sapling → maturing → bearing → declining
  • Fruit swap = uproot & restart maturation (real cost)
  • Tending pressure: establishment-heavy then low
  • Yield: seasonal pulses once mature
  • Pollution loss: full maturation investment — re-establishing eats the timer again
  • Investment payback: slow but compounding

Lifecycle stages SET

Three (or four) visible sprite states — cheap to make, tells the whole story at a glance:

  1. Sapling — placed; high water demand; no yield. Tile is committed.
  2. Maturing — visibly larger trees; still no yield. Maturation timer runs over multiple harvest cycles.
  3. Bearing — fruit visible in season; yield pulses into silos at harvest windows.
  4. Declining SEED — older trees, lower yield. Optional fourth stage; could be a quiet pressure to replant or just a passive decay.

Mechanic differentiators SET

  • Tile lock. Orchard tile is committed to "tree" until uprooted. No re-cropping each season. This is the core feel difference — orchards are a bet on the map.
  • Maturation delay. Several harvest cycles before any yield. Real-time order of magnitude TBD (tuning question), but the player feels the wait.
  • Pulse harvest. Output arrives in seasonal bursts, not a continuous flow. Logistics has to surge during harvest windows — natural co-op pressure point.
  • Different suitability profile. Heat map shape changes when toggled to "apples" vs "barley" — different water, soil, slope tolerances. Reuses the existing overlay infrastructure (Tropico-style).
  • Pollution amplifier. Pollution killing a mature orchard is a much harder loss than losing a barley field — re-establishing means eating the maturation delay again. This makes industrial's siting decisions hit agri harder than fields do — exactly the interference tension the project wants.
  • Lower steady-state tending. Once mature, manual-tending cadence is lower than fields. Frees agri's attention for expansion or new orchards.
  • Generic-orchard, recipe-by-fruit. Mirrors the malthouse pattern — one orchard building, fruit picked at placement (apple at first; pear, cherry, grape later as cheap variety adds). Switching fruit = uprooting.
Why this works without new systems
Every bullet above is a parameter on existing systems: a longer growth timer, a different heat-map profile, a larger footprint, a different yield curve. The agri player doesn't learn new verbs; they learn that the verbs they already have play out very differently when the thing they're growing is a tree.

Optional event hook SEED

Pest / blight events can disproportionately hit orchards (long-investment loss) — fits the "narrative events absorb soft external pressure" pillar from the events memo. Not v1; flag for the events doc when it's drafted.

3. The Cider Chain — Same Template, Swapped Stages

Cider follows the same seven-stage shape as the lager chain. Stage 1 swaps a field for an orchard. Stage 3 (malting) is skipped entirely — cider has no malt step. Stage 5 (brewery) becomes a cider-specific brewery building with a different machine line. Logistics, sales, and credit flow are unchanged.

Recipe SET

Cider ←
+ Apples (Orchard, Agri)
+ Water (main, Logistics)

Cleaner recipe than lager — only one agri input, no malting step. The simplicity is the point: it proves that not every drink walks through every industrial building.

Stages on the map

Stage 1 · Agri
Apple orchard on suitable tiles → fruit silo (pulse harvest in season)
Stage 2 · Logistics
Truck pickup at fruit silo → deliver apples directly to cider brewery (no malthouse stop)
Stage 3 · Industrial
Cider brewery (water + power-fed): apples + water → press → ferment → cider; biological waste (pomace) branches off; sewage piped out
Stage 4 · Logistics
Truck pickup at cider brewery → deliver cider to sales outlet or trading depot
Stage 5 · Business
Sales outlet sells locally; trading depot routes to demand regions where cider sells better than beer (demand model from slice 2 onward)

Inside the cider brewery — the 12-step production grid SEED

Real-world cider production is up to twelve distinct steps. Each one maps to a machine slot or sub-area on the brewery's interior grid — exactly the "Sandship-style internal grid" depth bar from the per-corp v1 memo. This is what gives the industrial player a genuine "building production" feel: not "place a brewery and watch it fill," but "lay out the line, pick the sub-recipe at each branching step, run a real cidery."

Source
Twelve-step process per Cider Making — From Traditional to Modern (Popa & Harte, Dept. of FSHN / MSU Product Center, Michigan Apple Committee). Real-world steps simplified here into machine-slot vocabulary; the count and ordering are honest to the source. PDF on Ambrus's desktop.
Step 1 · Harvest
Outside the brewery (agri side). Apples arrive at the input bay with variety tags — sweet / sharp / bittersweet / bittersharp — that affect downstream blending choices.
Step 2 · Sweating
Apples rest 7–10 days on a wooden/concrete platform until soft. Variety-dependent: Jonathan, Newtown, Rome Beauty skip this and press fresh. A storage sub-room, not a machine — and skippable based on what agri delivers. Cross-corp coupling for free.
Step 3 · Washing
Water-fed station; removes leaves, twigs, residue, surface bacteria. Couples to the logistics water main with a real demand spike at this stage.
Step 4 · Grinding
Grater mill turns fruit into fine pulp. Throughput/quality knob: finer pulp = more juice yield per apple but longer cycle.
Step 5 · Pressing
Plate press extracts the juice (now called "must"). Pomace (~3% of pulp + skins/seeds) branches off as biological waste — feeds directly into the existing fertilizer loop with agri.
Step 6 · Blending
Three valid placements: pre-grind, post-press, or post-fermentation. Later blending = more control over final quality but more buffer-tank storage required. Pure player decision point.
Step 7 · Testing
Sugar (Brix), acid, and pH check. In-game: a quality gate that flags off-spec batches and feeds back into recipe tuning. A QA station, not a transformation.
Step 8 · Fermentation
Fermenter machine — shared with the lager brewery. Sub-recipe choice: SO2-sterilized + cultured yeast (modern industrial path, predictable), or natural indigenous yeast (traditional path, slower and chancier but distinctive flavor). Temperature affects throughput.
Step 9 · Racking off
Drain to a second vessel, leaving sediment behind. Quiet step, but it claims a buffer tank slot for the duration.
Step 10 · Filter / fining
Two valid paths: closed-filter machine (fast, dry) or fining-additive station (gelatin / bentonite / pectic enzyme — slower, traditional). Both reach "clear cider"; the choice is part of the player's identity for this run.
Step 11 · Bottling
Sterile bottling line. Optional sub-steps: in-bottle sugar dose for natural carbonation, pasteurization to halt further fermentation. Forced CO2 injection is an alternative carbonation path here.
Step 12 · Storage
Required short maturation in cool, dark storage before shipping. This is the bridge to the aging doc — speculative long-aging hooks in here for premium cider variants.
Where the player actually decides — the control surface of the minigame
Most of the twelve steps are placement-and-connect. A handful are real recipe choices the player makes per cidery, per batch, or per recipe:
  • Sweating yes / no — gated by which apple variety agri delivers
  • Blending stage — pre-grind / post-press / post-ferment
  • Fermentation method — SO2 + cultured yeast vs. natural indigenous yeast
  • Filter vs. fining — equipment path
  • Carbonation — natural in-bottle vs. forced CO2
  • Pasteurize yes / no — interacts with aging; pasteurized batches lock the curve, unpasteurized continue evolving in storage
Six small forks on a stable layout. That's the game inside the building.
Bespoke first, copy-paste after — the chore problem solved
Twelve steps and six recipe forks would be brutal if the player had to lay it out from scratch every time. The brewery interior should support saving a working layout as a template / blueprint, then stamping it into the next cidery building. First run is the puzzle; second cidery onward is one click. Bespoke when you want it, copy-paste when you don't. SEED — proper UX for save / load / share blueprints belongs in a separate doc once the interior grid mechanics shake out.

The 12-step grid is the design vocabulary, not a v1 mandate. Many steps can ship auto-resolved (washing, racking, testing as silent QA) and only become player-placeable as tech-tree unlocks expose them. The full grid is the long-run depth ceiling, not the slice-2 floor.

What's new vs. lager — the honest scope
  • Agri: orchard building + apple recipe + maturation timer + variety tagging (sweet / sharp / bittersweet / bittersharp).
  • Industrial: cider brewery building + a set of 6–8 new interior machine types (sweating room, washer, grater mill, plate press, racking vessel, filter / fining station, bottling line, in-bottle carbonator). Fermenter shared with lager.
  • Logistics: nothing new structurally — pomace handoff reuses the existing biological-waste channel.
  • Business: nothing new structurally — sells a second product. Sub-recipe forks may surface as SKU variants later.
Bigger scope than the original "one new machine" framing — but Ambrus explicitly chose this depth, and almost every interior machine reuses cleanly into wine, perry, cherry cider, calvados, and the rest of the fruit-alcohol family. The art and code spend pays off across the whole side of the drink tree.

Variety expansion (later, no new logic)

  • Pear → perry. Same orchard building, different fruit recipe. Same brewery line.
  • Cherry → cherry cider. Same shape.
  • Grape → wine. Reuses press + fermenter; adds aging as a real new mechanic — flag for a dedicated wine doc, since aging changes the timing model.

4. Beer Varieties as Cheap Recipe Expansions

Inspirations only — not v1 scope. The pattern across these is that almost every beer style is a recipe-level variant of the lager pipeline, not a new pipeline. Expansions tend to add exactly one of three things:

  1. One new agri input (a new grain, a new hop variety)
  2. One new machine step (roasting, dry-hopping)
  3. A timer change (cold conditioning, longer aging, open fermentation)

Picking one of those three axes per variety keeps the expansion cheap and the simulation deeper without rewriting any system.

Axis: timer change · machines unchanged

Pilsner

A lager sub-recipe. Same machines as lager; specific noble-hop input plus a longer cold-conditioning timer. Adds nothing structurally — purely a numbers tune. Good first "variety" to ship because it costs almost nothing.

Axis: new machine step at the malthouse

Porter

Adds a malt-roasting recipe slot at the malthouse — produces "roasted malt" which feeds the brewery as an alternative to standard malt. Reuses the malthouse upgrade pattern that's already in the design ("upgradeable to handle multiple recipes simultaneously").

Axis: new machine step + extended grain inputs

Stout

Extends Porter with an additional unmalted grain input — typically roasted barley, sometimes oats. From an architecture standpoint: the second roasted-malt recipe at the malthouse plus an alternate agri crop (oats). Strong adjacency to porter — they share most of the chain.

Axis: new machine step at the brewery

IPA

Adds a dry-hop machine slot post-fermentation. Heavy hop input demand — a single IPA brewery can pressure agri's hop fields multiple times harder than a lager brewery. This is a great teaching moment for the cross-corp interference layer: agri suddenly has to specialise.

Axis: new agri input · brewery unchanged

Wheat beer (Hefeweizen)

Cheapest possible variety for agri to ship: alternate grain crop (wheat field) with its own suitability profile. Brewery line is unchanged — the malthouse handles wheat malting via a recipe variant. Pure "new field type" variety expansion.

Axis: timer change + event-coupled

Sour / Lambic

Open fermentation with very long aging. Best fit as an event-driven variety rather than a clean recipe — contamination risk, terroir, narrative hooks. Fits the events pillar from the narrative events memo. Saved for after the events system has shape.

The pattern that matters
Every variety above sits on one of three axes (input / machine / timer). If a future beer doesn't fit any of those three axes, that's the signal it's actually a different chain, not a variety — and it deserves its own doc.

6. What This Slice Teaches Each Corp

What playing through the cider chain — on top of the lager chain — adds to each player's mental model of the system.

CorpWhat cider teaches themWhat it deliberately doesn't
Agri Long-cycle commitment, fruit suitability heat map, pulse-harvest rhythm, real cost of pollution near a long investment Aging-based crops (wine grapes), multi-fruit orchards on one tile, automated harvest
Industrial The first real interior minigame: a 12-step grid with six recipe forks. Layout placement, sub-recipe choices, and blueprint-stamp reuse for second-cidery onward. The chain template generalises — not every drink needs a malthouse Aging timers, distillation, multi-recipe breweries (still per-chain in this slice)

Cross-corp consistency check
The cider chain only adds value if the four corps can run lager and cider concurrently without one chain starving the other. The shared scarcities — water, road capacity, sales-outlet throughput — are exactly what should start to feel tight when the second chain comes online. If they don't, the slice is too generous; if they're crushing, the slice is too lean. Tune water budgets and truck capacity here, not in the lager slice.

7. Open Questions

Q1. How long is the orchard maturation delay in real-time?
Has to feel meaningful (otherwise orchards are just "slow fields") but not punishing in a session that may only run an hour. Probably tied to harvest-cycle counts rather than wall-clock time. Tuning question.
Q2. Does declining ship in v1, or do orchards bear forever?
Declining adds replanting pressure but also a quiet decay system the agri player has to monitor. Could ship without it and add later as a tech-tree-gated realism toggle.
Q3. Is the orchard footprint 2×2 fixed, or scalable (3×3, 4×4 = small / medium / large orchard)?
Scalable orchards give agri an expansion knob without infinite tile-by-tile placement. Fixed-size is simpler for v1.
Q4. Does cider share a sales outlet sprite with beer, or get a "cidery" outlet variant?
Mechanically the outlet doesn't care what it sells. Visually, distinct outlet variants would help business players read the map at a glance. Cheap to do later as a reskin.
Q5. Pomace (apple pulp) — straight to the fertilizer loop, or a separate animal-feed branch?
Real-world pomace is animal feed. In-game, simplest is "treat as biological waste, route to fertilizer." A separate feed branch would only matter if livestock enters the design.
Q6. Can the lager brewery be expanded to handle cider as a second recipe later, or is per-chain locked-in?
Echoes 2026-05-01 Q5. Per-chain wins on map readability and machine layout stability; a "multi-product brewery" tech upgrade much later in the run could combine the two for veteran players. Not a v1 call.
Q7. Order of slices: cider before beer-variety, or beer-variety before cider?
Cider first introduces the orchard system (a real new building). Beer-variety first reuses everything (cheaper to ship, deepens the existing chain). Lean is cider first because the orchard is more memorable for early playtesters; beer-variety can dribble in afterward.
Q8. Does the heat map need a different visual style for orchards (perennial) vs fields (annual)?
Same overlay infrastructure can probably handle both with just a different color ramp per crop. Worth a quick visual test before committing.
Q9. How many of the 12 steps are exposed to the player at slice 2, vs auto-resolved or hidden behind tech-tree unlocks?
Lean: ship slice 2 with the visible spine — grinding, pressing, fermentation, bottling — and let washing, racking, testing run as silent QA inside the line. Sweating, blending, filter/fining, and carbonation surface as tech-tree unlocks that progressively reveal the recipe forks. Avoids overwhelming a first-time industrial player while leaving the depth ceiling intact.
Q10. Sub-recipe forks (SO2 vs natural, filter vs fining, etc.) — chosen per-batch, per-building, or per-recipe?
Per-batch is most flexible but heaviest UI. Per-building is simplest but loses the "two cideries running different styles" use case. Per-recipe is the middle path — the player saves a recipe with its forks baked in, then chooses which recipe to run per batch. Probably the right answer.
Q11. Blueprint / template system — minimum viable scope?
At a minimum: save layout to a slot, stamp slot into a new building. Stretch goals: share blueprints between players in co-op, version blueprints, mark recipe parameters as "locked" or "edit per use." Worth its own UX pass before building.

8. Scratch / Parking Lot

Half-formed thoughts
  • If wine grapes ship later, the orchard building probably becomes a "perennial crop" parent class — orchard, vineyard, hop-yard (hops are technically perennial too — interesting reframe).
  • The orchard's pollution sensitivity could be the prompt that pushes agri toward biotech sub-types in the corp tech tree (pollution-resistant cultivars).
  • Pulse harvest as a UI moment — when the orchard yields, a small "harvest ready" indicator pings the agri player. Could be the first system that gives agri a bursty interaction (vs the steady-trickle of fields).
  • Cyberpunk reskin path: vat-grown synth-apples replace the orchard sprite at a tech tier. Same lifecycle stages, cleaner aesthetic.
  • If the press machine is shared across cider, perry, cherry-cider, and wine, that's a strong candidate for the first "machine that shows up in many recipes" teaching moment for the industrial player.
  • Beer-variety axes (input / machine / timer) might be a more general design tool — apply it to spirits, wine, soft drinks. If it survives that test, it's a real principle.
  • Trading depot becomes much more interesting once there are two products: now there's a real "ship which to where" decision for the business player.
